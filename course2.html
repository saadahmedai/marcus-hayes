Module 01

Picking Your Channel Direction

Before choosing a niche from the list in Module 2, this module helps you figure out which direction makes sense for you specifically — based on your existing knowledge, your available time, and the income level you're targeting.

Three questions before you pick a niche

  • What markets do you already know something about? — You don't need to be an expert. You need to be able to research and explain a topic clearly. History, animals, finance, psychology — any of these work if you can spend an hour learning about a specific video topic.
  • How much time can you put in per week? — One to two hours per day is enough for Shorts. Long-form needs more. Be honest about this before choosing a production style.
  • Are you targeting speed to monetization or higher RPM? — Shorts gets you to monetization faster (10M views in 90 days). Long-form earns more per view once you're monetized. Most people start with Shorts then add long-form.

Which path is right for you

  • Shorts first: If you want to reach the YouTube Partner Program as quickly as possible and you're comfortable making 60-second videos consistently
  • Long-form first: If you want higher RPM from the start and you have 2–3 hours per week to produce 8–12 minute videos
  • Both from day one: Only if you've done this before. For beginners, splitting attention between formats at the start almost always results in mediocre output on both
💡

The fastest channel Marcus built used Shorts only for the first 30 days. Once monetized through the Shorts path, he added long-form content to increase RPM. That sequence — Shorts to monetize, long-form to scale — is the recommended starting point for most beginners.

Module 04

Competitor Research

Before making your first video, spend time studying what's already working in adjacent markets. The goal is not to copy — it's to understand which formats are proven, which hooks hold attention, and what a successful script actually looks like at a structural level.

Step 1 — Find the right channels to study

  • Search YouTube for the Format you want to use, not the Market you're entering
  • Look for channels in a different market using the same format — these are your structural references
  • Filter by view count: you want videos with 500K+ views — these have been validated by the algorithm
  • Avoid studying direct competitors in your exact niche — you want format references, not topic references

Step 2 — Extract transcripts

Go to youtubetotranscript.com and paste the URL of 2–3 top-performing videos from your reference channels. This gives you the full script text to analyze.

Look for: how long is the opening hook? What emotion does the first sentence trigger? How do they transition between sections? How do they close?

Step 3 — Build your own format map

  • Write the script structure as a numbered blueprint: Section 1 (hook), Section 2 (context), Section 3 (main content), Section 4 (close)
  • Note the average sentence length and vocabulary level
  • Identify their 3–5 most used transition phrases
  • Write the hook formula as a reusable template with blanks

This becomes your scripting reference for every video you produce.

Module 06

Channel Setup

Do this once before publishing your first video. Each of these steps takes a few minutes and the settings affect how YouTube categorizes and distributes your channel from day one.

The essential setup steps in order

  • Create a Google account using a brand-related name, not your personal name
  • Create your YouTube channel — youtube.com → profile → "Create a channel." Keep the name under 3 words. Brandable, memorable, topic-adjacent.
  • Design channel art in Canva (free) — banner size 2560×1440px. One or two colors. Clean and easy to read on both desktop and mobile.
  • Write your channel description — first 150 characters show in YouTube search results. Include your topic and what viewers get. Example: "Weekly videos on historical mysteries — explained clearly."
  • Set channel location to United States — YouTube Studio → Settings → Channel → Basic Info → Country. This affects which advertisers are matched to your content.
  • Set your default upload language to English — same section as above.
  • Enable auto-chapters — YouTube Studio → Settings → Channel → Advanced. Helps SEO and viewer navigation.
  • Build 3 videos before going public — upload all three as private, then publish simultaneously. A new visitor who finds your first video should have more to watch.
💡

Create playlists from day one. Group videos by topic. YouTube's autoplay feature extends watch sessions through playlists — which directly helps you reach the 4,000 watch-hour threshold needed for monetization on the long-form path.

Module 07

Thumbnail Basics

Your thumbnail is your ad. Before anyone watches a single second of your video, they see the thumbnail and make a decision. Getting this right has a disproportionate impact on how your channel grows.

What makes a thumbnail work

  • Readable on a phone at thumbnail size — hold your phone at arm's length and squint. If you can't read the text or identify the main subject, redesign it.
  • High contrast — white text on dark image, or dark text on light background. Avoid mid-tone combinations where text blends in.
  • Maximum 4 words of text — anything more than 4 words rarely gets read at thumbnail size. Choose the 3–4 words that create the most curiosity or tension.
  • One clear focal subject — a face, a dramatic scene, a single object. Thumbnails with too many elements compete with themselves for attention.
  • Emotional trigger in the first 0.5 seconds — the thumbnail should make someone feel something immediately: curiosity, shock, urgency, or recognition.

Creating thumbnails with your Google Flow images

  • Generate your scene images in Google Flow with thumbnail use in mind — dramatic lighting, clear subject placement, room for text overlay
  • Open Canva (free) → create a new design → YouTube Thumbnail (1280×720px)
  • Upload your Google Flow image as the background
  • Add text using a bold font (Canva has free options) — white text with a slight drop shadow reads on almost any background
  • Export and upload when setting your video live
📸 Image suggestion: a side-by-side comparison showing a weak thumbnail vs a strong thumbnail. Create two versions of the same video thumbnail in Canva — one with too much text and low contrast, one clean and bold.
